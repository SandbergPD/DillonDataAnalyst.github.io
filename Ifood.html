Delivery Decoded:

Analyzing the Flavorful Data of Delivery Trends

Just think, you're watching your favorite show or your favorite sports game, and you realize the food you just got at the supermarket just isn't as appealing as it was when you got it. And why not get food delivered? I can't be the only one that thinks like that! So I wanted to get an eye on some data and see how many others might be like me.

Queue Ifood and the dataset that is present on GitHub.

Ifood Dataset

Ifood is a company like Doordash that provides meal delivery services in Brazil. Gaining valuable insights from data and understanding the spending habits and customer base.

  • 67% of the spending variance can be explained by income
  • There are over 10,000 purchases made by the 60k-80k salary range category being the most prominent
  • Spending on premium or gold-valued products is more common the lower a customers income

    • *Note this project is actually modified from an iFood job interview case study given by the Brazilian equivalent of DoorDash, iFood. The data is 98% real, but slightly modified for educational purposes.

    Data-Driven Insights

    Primarily using Microsoft Excel for most of the data analysis I started by looking at who joined and when. There is a pretty even spread over the year of new customers joining the platform with an average of 186 new sign-ups monthly, but peaking in January. That's a strange time for there to be new sign ups. I wish there was additional information that we could look at to identify a deeper trend. Maybe some people saying "New Year, New Me" and trying to eat healthy and not knowing where to start. So an easy way to eat healthy is to buy healthy. Or possibly people got gift cards during the holidays and they are finally using them. We'll never know!

    Having looked at when people signed up, I decided I needed to look at the spending habits. Which lead me into seeing if there was a correlation between income level and total amount spend through the service. And there was! A 67% relationship between a person's salary and the amount that is spent with the service.

    The more people make, the more they spend on the service, but to a point. I wanted to dive a little bit deeper and see if there was something that was missing. Using SQL I was able to look at the data easier and make different income brackets for the customers.

    One added benefit of separating the customers into income brackets, I was able to clearly show the sheer number of purchases from the 60k-80k bracket.

    Using Tableau you can clearly see that the more people make, the more they spend but until over 100K a year. Peaking at 10,595 purchases in store for the 60K-80K bracket and bottoming out at 138 for the 100K-120K bracket. But there were fewer people in the higher salary bracket and I wanted to see how that affected the average purchase price. That being said, there is an average purchase of $74.00 for the 60k-80k bracket then drops to $35.54. A 52% drop in purchasing amount.

    But what could this mean? People that hit a level of income that they no longer use Ifood as regularly as before? That they are able to spend the time and the money going out to eat and indulging?

    Lastly, I was intrigued by the spending habits of the customers. What customers wanted to elevate their purchase by buying an extravagant brand or item? How did their income affect this spending, I was very surprised by the result!

    The data shows us that the lower the annual income of an individual, the higher the percentage of their money goes to the purchase of gold-labeled products. There is only a 29% relation between income and the percentage of that income being spent on top-tier products. This chart does not look into the average amount spent or the number of purchases that the people make.

    By making a pivot chart back in Excel we can see that the highest number of purchases by an income bracket was that of the 60k-80K group but the highest average number of gold purchases was seen in the 100k-120k group. We see that actually the more you make the less you spend on gold-labeled products relative to your income. Inversely we see that the higher income individuals buy more gold-labeled products, it just isn't a higher percentage of their income.

    Takeout Takeaways

    Overall Ifood is doing something right having over $1.24 million in sales, and attracting over 2000 customers to use its services. This data has really shown the importance a business model like this can have on an income bracket. I would have never thought that the lower someone's income the more that they would spend on high-tiered products, I initially thought the opposite. This was an informative project and one I enjoyed. Being able to use Excel, SQL, and Tableau on a project, changes how you can see the data as a whole.
